Color


In kindergarten art class my teacher would always be so curious as to why I would draw the sky purple or the grass brown. Well come to find out both my brother and I were diagnosed with color vision deficiency, or more communally referred to as - colorblindness. In all honesty it has not been huge hindrance on me, but it did open my eyes to how the world treats disabilities. The world is not built for people with vision or hearing impairments, or people with disabled mobility. When I decide I wish to build something, accessibility is always at the forefront. Great design can be used by everyone, and if I am creating something I want it to reach no short of greatness.

Family


My grandmother, Miriam Primrose Miller, was a mother to eight children, a grandmother to more than 30 grandchildren, and a great-grandmother to more than 40 great-grandchildren. After being diagnosed with dementia, she stayed the last few years of her life at my childhood home while I was growing up. She was one of the sweetest people I have ever met and the lessons she taught reside with me to this day. The final and greatest lesson she taught me is that family is your legacy. This not only applies to the family you are given, but the family that you make. I am proud to have made a great family of friends and loved ones and I am proud to have been her legacy, and proud of the legacy that I will one day leave behind.



Lost


Growing up an overweight kid teaches you many lessons in character. I was bullied a lot in school about my size. I always knew one day my weight would not determine who I was. I knew I would persevere, and that’s exactly what I decided to do. Sophomore year I decide I was no longer going to be overweight. After my mind was made up it was all over from there. I dieted, exercised, danced, fought, and sweated my way to losing 100 pounds in a year. Well come to find out losing weight doesn’t change you. Yeah, you are typically healthier and might live longer but what really must change is your mindset. Your confidence, it comes from within and no amount of dieting or exercise can change that. The years that followed I kept the weight off but more importantly I decided who I was going to be. I no longer let the bullies make me feel lesser, I finally let the real me shine. In the end I am glad I lost the weight but what I am really proud of is that I discovered how great the real me actually is, without the voices of bullies getting in my way.



Pride


September 16, 2017, after dealing with much pain in my heart and soul, I had realized it was finally time to be myself. I decided to finally tell the world that I was gay. I struggled with being gay for most of my teenage years knowing I was different, knowing what I was feeling was not of the typical teenage boy. Being from the south I knew being gay would not be the easiest thing to reveal to people, so I hid it away till I got out of high school. After getting out of that small town I grew up in and away from the homophobic people of my past I was finally able to be my true self. I will not say it has always been easy since coming out, I lost some of my closest loved ones and many cherished friends, but life would be way harder hiding from myself. I can say love is love without any second thoughts now and I am happier for that every day.

[EDITORS NOTE] I contemplated long and hard, weighing various advice on whether I should exclude this section from my ‘about me’ page. The reason being that there is a high potential for employers to disqualify me from a position given that I am gay. I see this more as a feature rather than a bug; I don’t think I would ever fit into a place that would account for my sexuality when weighing whether I am fit for a job or not. People’s careers make up much of their happiness or their suffering, and for my own happiness I would rather work a place where I can truly be myself.
